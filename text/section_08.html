Section 8: Blue Collar Jobs & Robotics

Ross:

Unlike past disruptions, it could be that blue collar working class jobs — trades, jobs that require intense physical engagement with the world — might be, for a little while, more protected. Paralegals might be in more trouble than plumbers. And how long that lasts depends entirely on how fast robotics advances?

Dario:

I think that may be right in the short term. Anthropic and other companies are building very large data centers. And one of the things about data centers is you need a lot of electricians and construction workers to build them. As more and more of the intellectual work is done by A.I., what are the complements to it? Things that happen in the physical world.

Now, in the longer run, robotics is advancing quickly. If you've seen a Waymo or a Tesla recently, I think we're not that far from the world of self-driving cars. And A.I. itself will accelerate it, because these really smart brains — one of the things they're going to be smart at is how do you design better robots.

Ross:

Is there something distinctively difficult about operating in physical reality that is very different from the kind of problems that A.I. models have been overcoming?

Dario:

Intellectually, I don't think so. Anthropic's model, Claude, was actually used to pilot the Mars Rover. We've generally found that piloting a robot is not different in kind from playing a video game — it's different in complexity. And we're starting to get to the point where we have that complexity.

What is hard is the physical form of the robot, handling the higher-stakes safety issues. You don't want robots literally crushing people. Dropping the baby, breaking the dishes. There are practical issues that will slow things down. But I don't believe at all that there is some fundamental difference between the cognitive labor that A.I. models do and piloting things in the physical world. Those are both information problems.

Ross:

So a robot butler is a reality in 10 years?

Dario:

It will be on a longer time scale than the genius-level intelligence because of these practical issues. But it is only practical issues, not fundamental. The brain of the robot will be made in the next couple of years. The question is making the robot body and making sure that body operates safely.

Explained Simply

In Simple Terms

Here's an ironic twist: the jobs that require physical labor — electricians, plumbers, construction workers — might actually be SAFER from AI disruption than desk jobs. At least for now. The reason is simple: AI lives in computers. It can write code, analyze documents, and draft legal briefs without a body. But it can't rewire your house. Yet.

The brain vs. body problem: Dario makes a clear distinction. The "brain" of a robot (the AI that controls it) will be ready in a couple of years. But the "body" — the actual physical machine that can safely move through your house, pick up objects, and not crush your cat — that's a much harder engineering challenge. Think of it this way: we've already built the driver, but we're still building the car.

The Mars Rover Example

Dario casually drops that Claude (Anthropic's AI) was used to pilot the Mars Rover. His point: controlling a robot is fundamentally just processing information and making decisions, the same thing AI already does. A robot arm isn't "harder" for AI than a chess game — it's just more complex. And AI is getting better at handling complexity very quickly.

The safety gap: The reason robot butlers aren't here yet isn't because AI can't figure out how to fold laundry. It's because a robot operating in your home has to be PERFECTLY safe. A robot that drops dishes 1% of the time is annoying. A robot that drops a baby 0.01% of the time is unacceptable. The engineering challenge is making the body reliable enough for the real world.

Why This Matters to You

If you work with your hands — trades, construction, physical labor — you're probably safe for the next 5-10 years. In fact, demand for these jobs might INCREASE because of all the data centers being built. But don't assume physical work is permanently safe. Once the robot body problem is solved, the same AI disruption that's hitting white-collar work will come for physical work too.
