Section 5: AI & Democracy

Ross:

You try and make the case that AI could be good for democracy and liberty around the world, which is not necessarily intuitive. A lot of people say incredibly powerful technology in the hands of authoritarian leaders leads to concentrations of power.

Dario:

I admit that I don't know that the technology inherently favors liberty. I think it inherently favors curing disease and economic growth. But I worry that it may not inherently favor liberty. But what I say is — can we make it favor liberty? Can we make the United States and other democracies get ahead in this technology?

The United States has been technologically and militarily ahead, and that has meant we have throw weight around the world, augmented by our alliances with other democracies. We've been able to shape a world that I think is better than the world would be if it were shaped by Russia or by China. Can we use our lead in A.I. to shape liberty around the world?

I've often worried that today through social media, authoritarians are kind of undermining us. Can we counter that? Can we win the information war? Can we prevent authoritarians from invading countries like Ukraine or Taiwan by defending them with the power of A.I., with giant swarms of A.I.-powered drones — which we need to be careful about?

But also think about the justice system today. We promise equal justice for all. But the truth is, there are different judges in the world. The legal system is imperfect. I don't think we should replace judges with A.I., but is there some way in which A.I. can help us to be more fair, to be more uniform? Can we somehow use A.I. to create something that is fuzzy, but where you can give a promise that it's being applied in the same way to everyone?

Explained Simply

In Simple Terms

Ross asks a tough question: why would super-powerful technology be good for freedom? Historically, powerful technology often helps dictators control people. Dario's honest answer is: AI doesn't automatically help democracy. It has to be deliberately used for that purpose. His argument is basically: if democracies build the best AI first, they can use that advantage to protect freedom worldwide.

The three ways Dario thinks AI could help democracy:
1. Military defense — AI-powered drones could protect countries like Ukraine and Taiwan from invasion without risking soldiers' lives
2. Information warfare — Counter authoritarian propaganda and social media manipulation
3. Equal justice — Make the legal system more consistent and fair by using AI to ensure everyone gets treated the same way

The Uncomfortable Part

Notice what Dario is actually saying: the best defense of democracy is to make sure democracies have the most powerful AI weapons and tools. That's basically an arms race argument dressed up in democratic language. He acknowledges the tension — "we need to be careful about" drone swarms, for instance. But his fundamental position is: someone is going to build this technology, and it's better if it's us than if it's China or Russia.

The justice system idea is interesting but tricky: Using AI to make courts more "uniform" sounds great in theory — everyone gets the same treatment regardless of which judge they get. But who decides what "fair" means? The AI's definition of fairness is whatever its creators program into it. And different people genuinely disagree about what justice looks like.

Why This Matters to You

This section reveals the political reality of AI development. It's not just a technology story — it's a geopolitics story. The country that leads in AI may effectively lead the world. Dario is making the case that this should be the US and its allies, not authoritarian regimes. Whether you find that reassuring or alarming probably depends on how much you trust the US government with that kind of power.
