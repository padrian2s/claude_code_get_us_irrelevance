Section 7: Human Touch, Law & Adaptation

Ross:

People will say, we've had A.I. that's better at reading a scan than a radiologist for a while. But there isn't job loss in radiology. People keep being hired. Doesn't that suggest that people will want the A.I. AND a human to interpret it, because we're human beings?

Dario:

It's going to be pretty heterogeneous. There may be areas where a human touch is particularly important. You go in and you're getting cancer diagnosed — you might not want HAL from 2001 to be the one to diagnose your cancer. That's just maybe not a human way of doing things.

But if we look at customer service, actually customer service is a terrible job and the humans who do it lose their patience a lot. And customers don't much like talking to them because it's a pretty robotic interaction honestly. Maybe actually it would be better for all concerned if this job were done by machines.

Ross:

Let's take the example of the law. I know a lot of lawyers who have looked at what AI can do in terms of legal research and brief writing and have said, yeah, this is going to be a bloodbath for the way our profession works right now.

Dario:

Law has a system of training and apprenticeship — paralegals, junior lawyers who do behind-the-scenes research for cases. Then it has the top tier lawyers who are actually in the courtroom. It seems really easy to imagine a world where all of the apprentice roles go away. And you're just left with the jobs that involve talking to clients, talking to juries, talking to judges.

Particularly if you froze the quality of the technology in place, there are over time ways to adapt. Maybe we just need more lawyers who spend their time talking to clients. Maybe lawyers become more like salespeople or consultants who explain what goes on in the contracts written by A.I. If we had enough time, that would happen. But reshaping industries like that takes years or decades, whereas these economic forces are going to happen very quickly. And it's not just happening in law — the same thing is happening in consulting, finance, medicine, and coding. It becomes a macroeconomic phenomenon.

Ross:

What preserves human agency is law and custom. You could replace the judge with Claude version 17.9. But you choose not to because the law requires there to be a human.

Dario:

I would argue that in many cases, we do want to stay in charge. That's a choice we want to make, even in some cases when we think the humans on average make kind of worse decisions. Society can only adapt so fast if it's going to be good.

Explained Simply

In Simple Terms

This section explores a crucial question: in which jobs will humans actually WANT to be replaced, and in which will they insist on keeping a human? The answer varies wildly. Getting a cancer diagnosis from a robot feels wrong. But dealing with a customer service bot that's actually patient and helpful? Most people would prefer that to an exhausted, frustrated human agent.

The law as a case study: Law has a clear hierarchy. At the bottom: paralegals and junior associates who do research, review documents, and write drafts. At the top: experienced lawyers who argue in court and negotiate deals. AI is already very good at the bottom-tier work. The "bloodbath" Dario describes is for those entry-level positions. But you can't replace the courtroom lawyer — because the law literally requires a human to stand before a judge.

The Macroeconomic Problem

Here's what makes this different from past disruptions: it's not just one industry being disrupted. It's law AND consulting AND finance AND medicine AND software AND customer service, all at the same time. When just one industry changes, people can move to another. When everything changes at once, where do you go? That's the "macroeconomic phenomenon" Dario is worried about.

Deliberate human retention: Ross makes a fascinating point — sometimes we'll CHOOSE to keep humans in charge even when AI is better. Not because the human makes better decisions, but because we believe certain decisions should be made by humans. You have a right to be judged by a human jury. You have a right to a human judge. These aren't efficiency decisions — they're values decisions.

Why This Matters to You

If you work in a field with a clear apprenticeship ladder (law, medicine, finance, consulting), the bottom rungs of that ladder are at risk. The irony is that senior positions might actually become MORE valuable — you still need a human face in the courtroom, across the negotiating table, sitting with a patient. But how do you GET to senior without the junior years? That's the training pipeline problem nobody has solved yet.
