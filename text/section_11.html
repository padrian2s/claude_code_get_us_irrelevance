Section 11: Domestic Risks & Civil Liberties

Ross:

What about the domestic landscape? You are building a tremendously powerful technology. What is the safeguard to prevent A.I. becoming a tool of authoritarian takeover inside a democratic context?

Dario:

That's one reason why I'm worried about the autonomous drone swarm. The constitutional protections in our military structures depend on the idea that there are humans who would, we hope, disobey illegal orders. With fully autonomous weapons, we don't necessarily have those protections.

But I actually think this whole idea of constitutional rights and liberty along many different dimensions can be undermined by A.I. if we don't update these protections appropriately. Think about the Fourth Amendment. It is not illegal to put cameras around everywhere in public space and record every conversation in a public space. You don't have a right to privacy in a public space.

But today, the government couldn't record all of that and make sense of it. With A.I., the ability to transcribe speech, to look through it, correlate it all — you could say, "Oh, this person is a member of the opposition. This person is expressing this view," and make a map of all 100 million people. You could make a mockery of the Fourth Amendment by the technology finding technical ways around it.

Is there some way of reconceptualizing constitutional rights and liberties in the age of A.I.? Maybe we don't need to write a new constitution. But do we expand the meaning of the Fourth Amendment? Do we expand the meaning of the First Amendment? And you have to do it just as the legal profession has to update in a rapid amount of time. Politics has to update in a rapid amount of time.

Explained Simply

In Simple Terms

This is one of the most important sections of the interview. Dario is pointing out that our constitutional protections were designed for a world without AI. They have loopholes that AI can exploit, even without anyone breaking the law. The scariest example: mass surveillance that's technically legal because it happens in "public spaces."

The Fourth Amendment problem, explained:
The Fourth Amendment protects you from "unreasonable searches." But it mainly applies to your private spaces — your home, your phone, etc. In a public park, on the street, in a cafe? You don't have a legal expectation of privacy.

Before AI: This was fine because no one could monitor every conversation in every public space. The data was too overwhelming.
After AI: AI can transcribe, analyze, and cross-reference every conversation captured on public cameras. Suddenly, "no privacy in public" means the government can build a complete map of who you are, what you believe, and who you associate with — all without breaking a single law.

The Autonomous Weapons Problem

Dario makes another chilling point about the military. One of the unwritten safeguards of democracy is that soldiers can refuse illegal orders. If a president ordered the military to fire on civilians, we'd hope that generals and soldiers would refuse. But a drone swarm has no conscience. It does what it's told. This removes one of the last checks on authoritarian misuse of military power.

The time problem again: Updating constitutional law is one of the slowest processes in any democracy. Supreme Court cases take years. Constitutional amendments take decades. AI capabilities are advancing in months. There's a fundamental mismatch between how fast the technology moves and how fast our legal protections can adapt.

Why This Matters to You

You don't need a dictator to lose your civil liberties to AI. All you need is the current legal system, which was written before AI existed, plus AI surveillance technology that's already being developed. The cameras are already there. The speech recognition is already there. What's missing is the AI that connects all the dots — and that's coming fast. Dario is calling for updating our legal protections before it's too late.
