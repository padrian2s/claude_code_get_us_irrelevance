Section 3: A Country of Geniuses in a Data Center

Ross:

One of the interesting things about your framing is that these intelligences don't have to be the kind of maximal godlike superintelligence that comes up in A.I. debates. You're basically saying, if we can achieve a strong intelligence at the level of peak human performance — and then multiply it. Your phrase is, "A country of geniuses."

Dario:

Have 100 million of them. Each a little trained, a little different, or trying a different problem. There's benefit in diversification and trying things a little differently. You don't have to have the full machine God. And indeed, there are places where I cast doubt on whether the machine God would be that much more effective than the 100 million geniuses.

I have this concept called the diminishing returns to intelligence. Economists talk about the marginal productivity of land and labor. We've never thought about the marginal productivity of intelligence. But if I look at some of these problems in biology — at some level, you just have to interact with the world. At some level, you just have to try things. At some level, you just have to comply with the laws or change the laws on getting medicines through the regulatory system. So there's a finite rate at which these changes can happen.

Now there are some domains like if you're playing chess or Go where the intelligence ceiling is extremely high. But I think the real world has a lot of limiters. So maybe you can go above the genius level. But sometimes I think all this discussion of "could you use a moon of computation to make an AI God" — they're a little bit sensationalistic and besides the point, even as I think this will be the biggest thing that ever happened to humanity.

Explained Simply

In Simple Terms

People often imagine AI becoming a single all-knowing god. Dario says that's not what matters. What matters is having the equivalent of 100 million really smart people (genius-level) working on problems simultaneously. Each one slightly different, tackling things from different angles. That's way more useful than one super-god AI.

Diminishing Returns to Intelligence: After a certain point, being smarter doesn't help that much. A chess engine benefits enormously from more intelligence because chess is a pure thinking game. But curing cancer? You still have to run lab experiments, wait for cells to grow, get FDA approval. No amount of extra IQ speeds up those physical-world bottlenecks.

The Real-World Speed Limit

This is a really important and often overlooked idea. Even if you had an infinitely smart AI, some things just take time. Medicine has to go through clinical trials. Laws have to go through legislatures. Buildings have to be physically constructed. Intelligence can speed up the THINKING part dramatically, but the real world has speed limits that no amount of brainpower can overcome.

Analogy: Imagine you're baking bread. A genius chef can design the perfect recipe instantly, but the dough still needs time to rise. That's the "diminishing returns to intelligence" — after a point, the bottleneck isn't thinking, it's the physical world.

Why This Matters

This is reassuring in two ways. First, it means we don't need to build a terrifying super-intelligence to get enormous benefits — "merely" genius-level AI, multiplied, is enough. Second, the real world's speed limits give us time to adapt. AI can think faster, but it still has to wait for experiments, approvals, and physical processes.
