Explained Simply

The Five Stages

This is one of the most important frameworks in the interview. Dario is frustrated that people collapse "AI is writing code" into one thing, when it's actually a long spectrum with wildly different implications at each stage.

The coding productivity spectrum — five stages:
Stage 1: AI writes 90% of lines of code. Already happened. But this is like saying "compilers write 100% of machine code." It sounds impressive but doesn't tell you much about whether programmers are needed.
Stage 2: AI writes 100% of lines of code. The human never touches actual code — they describe what they want and the AI produces it. Significant, but the human still architects, debugs, and decides.
Stage 3: AI handles 90% of end-to-end SWE tasks. Not just writing code, but setting up environments, running tests, writing documentation, handling deployments. The full job, not just the typing part.
Stage 4: AI handles 100% of today's SWE tasks. But humans move to new, higher-level work — like managers of AI systems. New jobs emerge that didn't exist before.
Stage 5: 90% less demand for software engineers. This is the real disruption. The total amount of human SWE labor the economy needs drops dramatically.

We are currently between Stages 1 and 2. People frequently mistake Stage 1 for Stage 5.

The Productivity Paradox

Dwarkesh raises a genuinely important challenge: if AI coding tools are so good, why don't we see a flood of incredible new software? Where's the renaissance? Dario's answer is honest: right now, the productivity gain is only about 15-20%. Six months ago it was 5%. That's real but not revolutionary yet. The exponential is still in its early stages where the numbers are small. The same way compound interest feels like nothing for the first few years and then suddenly your account doubles.

The study that should make you pause: In a controlled experiment, experienced developers using AI tools FELT 20% more productive but were actually 20% LESS productive. They completed fewer pull requests, not more. How do you square this with Dario saying "these tools make us a lot more productive"? His answer: Anthropic is under extreme competitive pressure, and they measure end output (model launches, revenue growth), not vibes. They can't afford to be wrong about whether the tools work. The discrepancy may be that the study measured experienced developers on FAMILIAR codebases — exactly where AI help matters least. The biggest gains come on unfamiliar tasks, large codebases, and boilerplate work.
"There is zero time for bullshit. There is zero time for feeling like we're productive when we're not."

This is the most convincing thing Dario says about coding productivity. It's not a benchmark or a vibe — it's a company in a fight for survival saying "these tools measurably help us ship faster." The proof is in the model launches and the revenue curve.
